Understanding Sustainability Metrics

Feedstock Sourcing Scoring Scale

Score Availability Description Feedstock Examples
0-2 Very Low Availability - Highly inconsistent supply, significant environmental impact, not renewable. High-impact virgin resources, seasonally dependent crops, non-renewable resources (e.g., coal-based feedstock).
3-4 Low Availability - Limited supply, moderate-to-high environmental impact, potential supply interruptions. Limited regional agricultural crops, niche bio-oils with limited availability.
5-6 Moderate Availability - Moderately reliable, renewable but may have moderate environmental concerns. Region-specific crops (e.g., energy crops like switchgrass), seasonal residues (e.g., forestry residues).
7-8 High Availability - Consistent supply, renewable with low-to-moderate environmental impacts. Common waste streams (e.g., agricultural residues, forestry by-products), regionally abundant materials.
9-10 Very High Availability - Highly consistent supply, renewable, low environmental impact, minimal resource competition. Municipal solid waste, used cooking oil, waste plastics, highly available residues or by-products.

Reference Standards for SAF Parameters

  • Carbon Intensity (CORSIA): Must achieve at least a 10% reduction in lifecycle GHG emissions compared to conventional jet fuel, targeting values below 89 gCO₂e/MJ, with optimal values between 20–40 gCO₂e/MJ.
  • Energy Density (ASTM D7566): SAF should meet the standard energy density of conventional jet fuels, approximately 42.8 MJ/kg, with acceptable SAF values between 40–44 MJ/kg.
  • Sulfur Content (ASTM D7566): SAF sulfur content should remain below 15 ppm to limit sulfur emissions and enhance environmental sustainability.
  • Flash Point (ASTM D7566): Minimum flash point requirement is 38°C to ensure fuel safety and compatibility with aviation handling.
  • NOx and CO Emissions: SAF should achieve a 15–25% reduction in NOx and CO emissions compared to conventional jet fuels.

What is EROI (Energy Return on Investment)?

EROI measures the efficiency of fuel production by comparing the energy output of the fuel to the energy input needed to produce it. For SAF, a higher EROI means the fuel provides more energy than it takes to produce, making it more sustainable and efficient.

Why EROI Matters:

  • High EROI (above 5): Indicates efficient production, where SAF provides much more energy than it consumes, supporting sustainability.
  • Moderate EROI (3-5): Shows some efficiency, but production could be improved.
  • Low EROI (below 3): Suggests that producing SAF consumes almost as much energy as it generates, reducing its sustainability.

How to Calculate:

To find EROI, divide Total Energy Output (MJ) by Total Energy Input (MJ) across all stages (feedstock production, transport, processing, and distribution).

EROI = Total Energy Output Total Energy Input

A strong EROI score highlights SAF’s potential as a sustainable fuel, ensuring it produces energy efficiently while meeting aviation needs.
